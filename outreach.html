Global Outreach & Medical Anthropology

My professional practice has been shaped by fieldwork and service in the Dominican Republic, Ghana, and Peru. Each setting offered a different perspective on how geography, history, culture, and economics influence health and access to care. These experiences strengthened my commitment to health equity and deepened my belief that effective healthcare must be culturally and contextually grounded.

Dominican Republic – Rural Mountain Clinics

In the Dominican Republic, I worked with an interdiscplinary team, including the Peace Corp, to provide care in remote mountain communities. We often traveled across challenging terrain to reach families whose access to healthcare was limited and unpredictable. While the delivery of supplies and clinical support was critical, the work also illuminated the indispensable role of local knowledge, relationships, and community-based systems of care.

Ghana – Fertility and Nutrition Education

In rural Ghana, I accompanied United States Military Academy cadets engaged in cultural immersion training and collaborated with the NGO Global Mamas. In service to the local community, I designed and delivered maternal health education focused on fertility and nutrition during pregnancy and the postpartum period. I also brought my five-year-old daughter with me, believing that early exposure to service, cultural humility, and cross-cultural connection is a powerful form of education.

This work required a culturally responsive approach that respected gender norms, acknowledged a blended landscape of spiritual and biomedical healthcare belief systems, and accounted for pervasive economic hardship. Because most families could not afford specialized foods or supplements, nutritional education was intentionally adapted to the local food system—ensuring that recommendations were both culturally grounded and economically attainable.

Peru – Indigenous Healing Traditions

As part of my integrative health studies, I traveled to Peru’s Sacred Valley to learn from Indigenous healers (curanderos) and to participate in rituals such as despacho offerings. These ceremonies emphasized reciprocity, gratitude, and right relationship with land and community.

While my practice is grounded in evidence-based care, these experiences reaffirmed that healing is more than a set of interventions; it is also about meaning, connection, and belonging. The Sacred Valley became, for me, a place where landscape, spirituality, and health were inseparable.
